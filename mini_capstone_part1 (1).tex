\subsection*{Executive Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Executive Summary}

\noindent\textbf{One-sentence takeaway.} I built a real image-level deepfake detector that performed strongly on benchmark data, but hard cases such as Gandhi taking a selfie, Photoshop-style portrait edits, and AI-edited people images showed me that pixel-level detection is not the same as authenticity.

\vspace{0.5em}

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.32\textwidth}
\centering
\summarycard{softgreen}{What comes next}{

}

My larger capstone goal is to build a detector for \textbf{AI-generated and AI-manipulated images and videos}. I started with images first because that was the most practical way to build a strong first layer, deploy it, and study its limits before adding video and more complex evidence.

The first detector worked well on its benchmark. The best preserved model was an \textbf{EfficientNetV2-B0} classifier trained on OpenForensics. In the saved experiment artifacts, it reached \textbf{0.9651 validation accuracy}, \textbf{0.9919 validation precision}, and \textbf{0.9824 validation AUC}. On a harder held-out test set, the score dropped to \textbf{0.8577 accuracy}. That already suggested that benchmark success does not automatically mean a detector is trustworthy once the conditions become messier.

The project changed because the original OpenForensics setup mainly covered \textbf{real and fake face images}. That was a strong first benchmark, but it was too narrow for the full capstone pipeline I actually want. It did not cover enough non-face images, Photoshop-style edits, AI-edited object scenes, or later video cases. That is why the data changed along with the pipeline.

To start that redesign, I switched to a broader image dataset and created a first pilot in Google Colab. In the new notebook, I mounted Google Drive, downloaded \textbf{Tiny-GenImage} from Hugging Face, created a balanced pilot subset, and trained the first \textbf{EfficientNet-B0} model in the new workflow. I limited that run to \textbf{4,000 training images} and \textbf{1,000 validation images} to make the first iteration of the new pipeline realistic under time and storage constraints.

That first pilot already produced a usable baseline. After \textbf{8 epochs}, the run reached a final \textbf{training loss of 0.0327}, \textbf{validation accuracy of 0.7710}, and \textbf{validation AUC of 0.8503}. Those numbers are lower than the older OpenForensics benchmark, but they come from a broader and more realistic image setup rather than a narrower face-forensics benchmark.

The main data strategy is now much clearer:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{OpenForensics} remains useful for the original face-forensics stage and for later semantic consistency work on faces.
    \item \textbf{Tiny-GenImage} gives the redesigned image pipeline broader coverage of AI-generated general images rather than only face cases.
    \item \textbf{Later additions} will need edited-image and video benchmarks so the shield can cover more of the real problem.
\end{itemize}

\callout{Why this matters for a general audience}{
The real value of the project is not only that I trained a detector. The value is that I built one, deployed it, tested it on hard cases, and used those failures to define a stronger long-term capstone about trustworthy image and video detection.
}

\subsection*{Academic Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Academic Abstract}

This mini-capstone documents the first completed iteration of a broader capstone whose long-term goal is an AI-generated media detector for both \textbf{images and video}. I began with the image branch and built a deployable AI-generated image detector using OpenForensics and transfer learning with \textbf{EfficientNetV2-B0}. The preserved pipeline standardized RGB loading, resizing to \textbf{256\(\times\)256}, normalization, augmentation, and thresholded sigmoid-based inference. Across the saved model comparisons, EfficientNetV2-B0 was the strongest preserved baseline, achieving \textbf{0.9651 validation accuracy}, \textbf{0.9919 validation precision}, and \textbf{0.9824 validation AUC}; on a harder held-out test set, performance dropped to \textbf{0.8577 accuracy}, indicating sensitivity to distribution shift and post-processing.

The most important outcome of the project was not only the deployed detector, but the limitation revealed through qualitative stress testing. A historically impossible but visually plausible image of Mahatma Gandhi taking a selfie, together with Photoshop-style portrait edits and AI-edited people images, showed that image-level detection and authenticity assessment are not equivalent tasks. I therefore redesigned the next stage as a layered AI Shield architecture combining low-level forensic modeling, semantic consistency analysis, contextual claim verification, provenance analysis, and later video extension. I also completed the first training pilot of the new image pipeline in Google Colab on a balanced Tiny-GenImage subset of \textbf{4,000} training images and \textbf{1,000} validation images; after \textbf{8 epochs}, that pilot reached a final \textbf{training loss of 0.0327}, \textbf{validation accuracy of 0.7710}, and \textbf{validation AUC of 0.8503}. The current work therefore establishes both a finished first detector and a concrete second-iteration image pipeline.

\section{Problem Statement and System Goal}

My larger capstone goal is an \textbf{AI-generated media detector} for both images and video. The practical constraint is that I could not build a fully general image-and-video system all at once while also keeping the work honest and reproducible. I therefore started with the image branch and asked a narrower technical question:

\begin{quote}
Can I build, compare, evaluate, and deploy a strong image-level detector for manipulated or AI-generated media under realistic conditions?
\end{quote}

The reason that question matters is that many detectors work well on clean benchmark data but are less convincing when the input is compressed, resized, reposted, edited again, or simply out of the data distribution they were trained on. In other words, benchmark accuracy is not the same thing as trustworthiness in the wild.

At a high level, the final system I want is an \textbf{AI Shield} that can combine multiple kinds of evidence:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item low-level forensic traces,
    \item semantic consistency,
    \item contextual verification,
    \item provenance and source history,
    \item and later, temporal evidence for video.
\end{enumerate}

But before I could justify that larger architecture, I needed to build one strong first layer and see where it broke.

\section{Why the First Dataset Was Not Enough}

The first pipeline used \textbf{OpenForensics} as its main data source. That was a reasonable starting point because OpenForensics is a serious benchmark for face-forgery detection and segmentation in the wild. It gave me a large, labeled real-vs-fake image problem with preserved train/validation/test splits and enough scale to make model comparison meaningful.

At the same time, OpenForensics covers a \textbf{narrower slice of the authenticity problem} than the final capstone requires. Its practical center of gravity is still face-related real/fake imagery. That means it is strong for questions like:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item does this face image look manipulated,
    \item is there evidence of face-specific artifact patterns,
    \item can a CNN separate the two classes on this benchmark.
\end{itemize}

It is much weaker for questions like:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item what if the image is not a face image at all,
    \item what if it is a general AI-generated object or scene,
    \item what if it is a Photoshop-style edit rather than a benchmark face forgery,
    \item what if it is semantically plausible in pixels but absurd in context,
    \item what if the medium is video rather than a single image.
\end{itemize}

So OpenForensics was a strong \textbf{first benchmark}, but it did \textbf{not} cover the full pipeline I ultimately need. That is exactly why the data had to change when the pipeline changed.

\subsection{Mathematical formulation of the first classifier}

The first detector is a binary classifier. Let
\[
x \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times256\times256}
\]
be an RGB image. The EfficientNet backbone maps the image to an internal representation and finally to a scalar logit
\[
z = f_{\theta}(x).
\]
The probability that the image belongs to the positive class is obtained with the sigmoid function:
\[
p(y=1\mid x) = \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}.
\]

Training minimizes binary cross-entropy. For a true label \(y\in\{0,1\}\), the loss for one example is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}}(y,p) = -\left[y\log(p) + (1-y)\log(1-p)\right].
\]

This matters conceptually because the classifier is not learning ``truth'' in the broad sense. It is learning a decision boundary between the two classes represented in the training data.

\subsection{Deployment}

The first forensic pipeline was also deployed as a Streamlit application. At the same time, deployment introduced a practical lesson: a working detector is still only one layer of the broader authenticity problem.

\section{Hard Cases and Why the Project Shifted}

The project changed most clearly when I began stress-testing the detector on cases that looked plausible but were conceptually more difficult. The image of \textbf{Mahatma Gandhi taking a selfie} became the clearest example because it is visually plausible enough to fool a naive realism detector while being historically impossible. But Gandhi was not the only important case. As I thought more seriously about the longer-term capstone, I realized that \textbf{Photoshop-style edited portraits}, \textbf{AI-edited photographs of people}, and other visually convincing edits reveal the same larger issue: image realism and authenticity are not the same thing.

That shift is the reason the project moved from ``deepfake detector'' to \textbf{AI Shield}. A detector that only judges pixels may still miss cases where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item the content is semantically inconsistent,
    \item the image fits the benchmark but not the claim,
    \item the source history is weak,
    \item or the medium is video instead of a single image.
\end{itemize}

\section{Iteration 2: Redesigned Image Pipeline Pilot}

\subsection{Why Tiny-GenImage}

To begin the redesign, I needed broader image data than OpenForensics. Tiny-GenImage gave me a practical way to start the new image pipeline because it contains more general real/fake image cases rather than mostly face-centered ones. I ran the new pilot in Google Colab and used Google Drive for storage and checkpoints.

The first practical subset was deliberately small:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{4,000} training images,
    \item \textbf{1,000} validation images,
    \item balanced real/fake sampling.
\end{itemize}

That choice was not about lowering ambition. It was about building the new pipeline in a way I could actually run, debug, checkpoint, and interpret.

\section{The New Low-Level Phase: EfficientNet + VAE}

The immediate next step in the redesigned image pipeline is to expand the low-level forensic phase from a single classifier into a \textbf{two-branch module}:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item a supervised classifier branch,
    \item and a variational autoencoder (VAE) anomaly branch.
\end{enumerate}

The classifier branch asks:
\begin{quote}
Does this image look like the real or fake classes I have already seen?
\end{quote}

The VAE branch asks a related but different question:
\begin{quote}
How well does this image fit the learned structure of authentic images?
\end{quote}

\subsection{VAE mathematics}

A variational autoencoder contains an encoder and a decoder. The encoder maps an input image \(x\) to a latent distribution with mean \(\mu(x)\) and variance \(\sigma^2(x)\). A latent variable is sampled as
\[
z = \mu(x) + \sigma(x)\odot\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I).
\]
The decoder then reconstructs the image as \(\hat{x} = g_{\phi}(z)\).

The standard VAE objective combines a reconstruction term and a KL-divergence term:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}}(x) = \underbrace{\|x-\hat{x}\|^2}_{\text{reconstruction}} + \beta\,\underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}\big(q_{\theta}(z\mid x)\;\|\;\mathcal{N}(0,I)\big)}_{\text{latent regularization}}.
\]

In plain language:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item the reconstruction term asks whether the model can rebuild the image well,
    \item the KL term keeps the latent space smooth and structured,
    \item and high reconstruction error can become a useful anomaly signal.
\end{itemize}

For this project, the plan is to train the VAE primarily on authentic images so that suspicious reconstructions can serve as an additional forensic signal next to the classifier probability.

The four phases are best understood as a \textbf{fused evidence pipeline}, not as four isolated models that never talk to each other. In the current design:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item the low-level forensic phase produces a classifier score \(p_{\mathrm{cls}}\) and, after the VAE is added, an anomaly score \(a_{\mathrm{vae}}\),
    \item the semantic phase produces a semantic inconsistency score \(s_{\mathrm{sem}}\) together with an attention map,
    \item the context phase produces a claim-consistency score \(s_{\mathrm{ctx}}\),
    \item and the provenance phase produces a provenance trust score \(s_{\mathrm{prov}}\).
\end{itemize}

The low-level forensic score itself is already two-branch in the redesigned pipeline:
\[
s_{\mathrm{forensic}}(x) = \alpha\, p_{\mathrm{cls}}(x) + (1-\alpha)\, a_{\mathrm{vae}}(x),
\]
where \(\alpha\in[0,1]\) controls how much weight is given to the supervised classifier versus the anomaly detector.

The final AI Shield output is then a weighted fusion of the phase scores:
\[
S(x,c,m) = w_1 s_{\mathrm{forensic}}(x) + w_2 s_{\mathrm{sem}}(x) + w_3 s_{\mathrm{ctx}}(x,c) + w_4 s_{\mathrm{prov}}(x,m),
\]
with \(w_1+w_2+w_3+w_4=1\). Here \(c\) denotes an optional claim or caption and \(m\) denotes optional metadata / provenance evidence.

In practice, the system does not have to force a binary answer for every input. A simple deployment policy is:
\[
\text{fake/manipulated if } S\ge \tau_H, \qquad
\text{real if } S\le \tau_L, \qquad
\text{uncertain otherwise},
\]
where \(\tau_H > \tau_L\). The purpose of the later phases is therefore not to overwrite the first detector, but to add new evidence when the image-only signal is not enough.

\section{Modeling Professional Process}

The most professional thing I did in this project was \textbf{not} to promise the whole final system too early. The process that ended up working was more realistic:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item scope a manageable first detector,
    \item train and compare models on a real benchmark,
    \item deploy something usable,
    \item stress-test it on cases that matter,
    \item and then let the failures redefine the next architecture.
\end{enumerate}

That sequence is reflected directly in the artifact trail. The older repository captures the first formulation of the project as a deployed OpenForensics-based detector. The newer repository captures the next formulation: a Colab workflow, a broader image dataset, and the beginning of a phase-1 module that is no longer only ``one more classifier.'' The point is not that the first repository was wrong. The point is that it documented the first version of the problem well enough that I could see where it stopped being enough.

A second professional-process lesson was that dataset choice is part of model design. OpenForensics was good enough to complete a serious first detector, but it was mainly a real/fake \emph{face-image} benchmark. Once I decided the real capstone question was broader than face forensics, the data strategy had to change too. The move to Tiny-GenImage was therefore not a random dataset swap; it was a correction in problem definition.

\section{Use of Generative AI}

Generative AI was part of the process, but not as a substitute for experimentation or evidence. I used it in three main ways. First, I used it to clarify model ideas while I was deciding how to redesign the project: for example, how a variational autoencoder would complement a supervised classifier, or how an attention-based semantic layer would differ from a forensic classifier. Second, I used it to reason through practical decisions such as dataset choice, Colab workflow, checkpointing, and how to stage the new pipeline so that I would not get stuck in archive downloads or over-scoped plans. Third, I used it to improve the writing by helping me distinguish what belongs in the executive summary, what belongs in the technical work product, and what belongs in process documentation.

The important boundary is that I did \textbf{not} use AI as a source of unverified experimental evidence. Whenever AI suggested an interpretation, I checked it against the actual artifacts: the old benchmark plots, the new training curves, the confusion matrix, the false positives and false negatives, the repository state, and the deployed app. In other words, AI helped me think and communicate more clearly, but the evidence in the report still comes from the work I actually ran.

\section{Strategies for \#navigation}

The most important navigation move was recognizing that ``image authenticity'' and ``face-forensics benchmark performance'' are not the same problem. At the beginning, that distinction was easy to blur because the first detector looked strong on saved OpenForensics metrics. The navigation problem was not simply logistical. It was epistemic: I had to decide whether the first detector really answered the problem I cared about, or whether it only answered the problem the benchmark made easiest.

The Gandhi case, Photoshop-style edits, and AI-edited portrait cases mattered because they made that navigation question concrete. They turned an abstract worry into evidence. After that, the project became easier to navigate, because I could decompose the new system into phases instead of trying to jump straight to a complete image-and-video platform.

A second navigation move was practical rather than conceptual. When the broader GenImage archives turned out to be too large and awkward for the pace of this mini-capstone, I did not keep forcing the data plan out of stubbornness. I used Tiny-GenImage as a deliberate pilot dataset instead. That is an example of good navigation: changing the route without abandoning the destination.

\section{Challenges and How I Responded}

\subsection{Challenge 1: The original benchmark was too narrow}
The first detector was strong, but the benchmark mainly covered real/fake face imagery. That meant I had evidence that I could build a real detector, but not yet evidence that I had the right detector for the broader capstone. I responded by reframing the project and changing the data strategy instead of pretending the old benchmark already solved the larger problem.

\subsection{Challenge 2: Data volume and infrastructure friction}
The project repeatedly ran into practical problems that were not glamorous but were very real: large datasets, archive structure, Colab runtime resets, Drive mounting, and the need to checkpoint experiments so I would not lose progress. I responded by using balanced pilot subsets, saving intermediate datasets to disk, and treating reproducibility as part of the project rather than as a convenience.

\subsection{Challenge 3: Interpreting results honestly}
It would have been easy to keep repeating the best OpenForensics validation numbers and call the project done. I did not want to do that. The held-out drop, the Gandhi failure case, the Tiny-GenImage false positives, and the new pilot confusion matrix all forced me to interpret model performance more carefully. I responded by using plots, threshold reasoning, and failure inspection instead of relying on one metric.

\section{Additional Learning Beyond the Work Product}

A substantial part of the learning in this mini-capstone is not reducible to the final detector itself. I learned how to think about benchmark fit, why score calibration and threshold choice matter, how anomaly detection differs from classification, why later semantic layers need different data from the first forensic layer, and why provenance cannot be treated as ``just metadata'' if it is going to be useful. I also learned a practical workflow lesson: if the dataset and the problem statement are mismatched, it is better to correct the mismatch early than to keep pushing a pipeline that is becoming less and less relevant.

The project improved most when I used outcomes that forced me to define the \emph{right} problem, collect the \emph{right} evidence, and present the work in a way that was honest for different audiences.
